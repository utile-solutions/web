Why your app feels cramped on mobile

The issue


You design and build on a desktop. Your product looks spacious and clean at 1440px. You check it on mobile, it renders without breaking, and you ship it.


Six months later, your mobile engagement is 40% lower than desktop. The product "works" on mobile — it just feels wrong. Cramped. Hard to use.


Why desktop spacing doesn't translate to mobile


On desktop, generous padding creates breathing room. The same padding on mobile eats into the content area.


A card with 32px padding on desktop uses 64px of the total width for empty space. At 375px viewport width, that's 17% of the entire screen consumed by padding alone.


Typography scales down, but line lengths don't adjust proportionally. Text that reads well at 600px column width becomes exhausting at 300px.


Touch targets that are fine with a mouse cursor — 32px buttons, tight icon links — become unusable with a thumb.


The specific issues to look for


**1. Touch target size** — Apple's HIG recommends minimum 44x44px touch targets. Google recommends 48x48px. Anything smaller creates mis-taps.


**2. Text container width** — On mobile, text should never run the full viewport width. 16px padding on each side (32px total) is a baseline minimum. 20px is better.


**3. Vertical rhythm on small screens** — Sections that have 80px vertical padding on desktop should have 48px on mobile. The rhythm should tighten, not stay fixed.


**4. Icon-only navigation** — Bottom navigation bars with small icons and tiny labels are common. Users over 40 consistently misidentify them or miss-tap.


The fix


1. **Define mobile-specific spacing tokens** — Don't just reduce desktop values. Design the mobile experience intentionally.

2. **Audit touch targets** — Use Chrome DevTools mobile emulation and click through every interactive element.

3. **Test with your actual thumb** — Put the phone in your non-dominant hand. Use your thumb. If you can't reach a target, users can't either.

4. **Increase line-height on mobile** — 1.6–1.7 line-height improves readability on small screens significantly.


The payoff


A well-spaced mobile experience doesn't just look better — it communicates care. Users feel respected when the product was designed for how they actually use it.
